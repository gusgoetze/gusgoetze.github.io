### Training Philosophy & Schedule
This wasn‚Äôt my first marathon. I ran my first in 2023, but between October 2023 and May 2024, I focused on strength training‚Äîcaloric surpluses, heavy lifts, and low reps. To maintain some level of cardio fitness, I ran two miles on the treadmill before each lifting session. Ten miles per week wasn‚Äôt much, but it kept my legs from completely resetting.

Then, in May, marathon training began. I shifted my approach entirely‚Äîlifting no longer centered around strength; instead, it was about complementing my running performance. Every decision‚Äîcross-training, recovery, warm-ups, diet‚Äîwas made with one goal: run faster, run stronger.

### Mileage Progression & Strength Reduction
My mileage climbed steadily‚Äîstarting at 23 miles per week and increasing by 6% per week until peaking at 50 miles in Week 16.

![Weekly Mileage](/assets/img/Marathon/weeklyMileage.png)

In the early weeks (when weekly mileage hovered in the 20s and 30s), I maintained most of my major lifts‚Äîbenching in the mid-200s, squatting in the low 300s, deadlifting in the low 400s. But as mileage ramped up, strength declined. Part of this was intentional. Trying to ego lift the equivalent of a baby elephant was an easy way to get injured.

The trade-off was worth it ‚Äî I was running faster and more efficiently.

### Heart Rate Data: Zone 2 or Nah?

They say Zone 2 running is the key to building an anaerobic base, and while I agree, I also think Zone 2 is as much a feeling as it is a number. It‚Äôs about running comfortably‚Äîbeing able to talk without gasping. According to my Garmin, that landed me in Zone 3 most of the time, with an average pace of 8:30-9:30 min/mi.

![Heart rate zone chart](/assets/img/Marathon/hrZoneTime.png)

* **Zone 2:** 12.3% of my time. Less than most running blogs would recommend
* **Zone 5:** 4 hours, 36 minutes- courtesy of my Friday "balls to the wall" runs

My absolute minimum heart rate on a run? 50 bpm on July 14. I started this day at 5:21 AM, when the weather was cool and cloudy. In fact, just 51 seconds in, my heart rate spikes to 102 bpm and then ends up averaging 126 bpm for the entire 14.06 mile run.

![Route map of my lowest heart rate run](/assets/img/Marathon/lowestHrMap.png)

My highest heart rate recorded was 176 bpm on September 13, 2024. On this day, I ran 6.03 miles at a 6:43 min/mi pace. It was 70 degrees with 64% humidity. In that last kick, when my heart rate peaked, I was running at a 5:55 min/mi pace.

In this map of my run, green means lower heart rate, red is higher heart rate. I was really pushing it!

![Route map of my highest heart rate run](/assets/img/Marathon/highestHrMap.png)

### Speed Analysis

My fastest mile was on September 20, when I clipped a mile at a 6:30 min/mi pace on a 10 mile run. 

My top speed, however, was 13.44 mph or a 4:28 min/mi pace. I probably held this only for a few seconds, likely at the end of a Friday run. Needless to say, I didn't do that.

My slowest mile was run at a 9:39 min/mi pace, on October 9, just 4 days before the marathon. On that run, which lasted 39 minutes and 3 seconds, my heart rate was in true Zone 2 for 95% of the time.

### Feed The Beast

Marathon training burns a ridiculous amount of calories. Especially with strict cross training and frequent 2-a-day workouts, you'll easily find yourself in a caloric deficit if you don't intentionally fuel up.

* **Lowest calories burned:** 339 kcal (easly 2.35 mile shakeout)
* **Most calories burned:** 2,850 kcal (20.04 miles in 75 degree heat)
* **Average calories burned per run:** 933 kcal

This doesn't count ~800 kcal I'd burn every spin session, the ~600 kcal I'd burn every weightlifting session, and the ~2000-2500 kcal I'd burn just by going about life.

### Reflecting on The Journey

Marathon day was amazing. In my first marathon, I got emotional when I saw mile 26 signs above me, knowing that I just had to kick an extra 0.2 miles to cross the finish line. This year, however, I was much more emotional on September 22. On this 75 degree Sunday, with a steady, light rain coming down, I logged my final 20 mile run of the training block. It was peak week. The third of 3, 50 mile weeks. From here on, for the next 3 weeks, I'd be tapering. It was only going to get easier. As the rain came down, raindrops mixed with tears. Subtle weeps, juxtaposed with conspicuous "Let's GO's" from me, to me, occupied my headspace. I was so proud. The hard work was done.

Training is the hard part. Race day is the celebration. I truly believe anyone can run a marathon. When you push yourself, find your limits, and grind it out, the results are beautiful.

While I'm proud of the medal and the finish time achieved, I'm more proud of the 113 runs that got me there.
